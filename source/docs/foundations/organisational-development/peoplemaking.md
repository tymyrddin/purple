# Book review: (New) Peoplemaking

![Peoplemaking](/_static/images/foundations-peoplemaking.png)

Virginia Satir's "Peoplemaking" (1972), revised as "The New Peoplemaking" (1988), is where the
[survival stances and congruence](satir-core.md) come from, though the book never mentions an organisation. It is a
family therapist's account of how people learn to communicate, written for parents rather than managers, and warm where
the Weinberg books are wry. Everything the later work borrows, the stances, congruence, resistance read as information,
sits in this book first, in its original domestic setting. Carrying it into a security team is left entirely to the reader, which
is both the book's limit and the reason it still reads fresh.

## The pot of self-worth

Satir's central image is the pot. Everyone carries a container of self-worth that runs high or low, and the level sets
what a person can do under pressure: someone whose pot is low cannot easily hear a criticism, deliver bad news, or admit
a mistake, because there is no reserve to absorb the hit. She insists the level is learned rather than fixed, which is
the hopeful part. For security the pot explains something the stances only describe. An engineer who buries a mistake, a
manager who cannot say "I got that wrong in the design review", a team that meets every finding as an attack, are often
not defensive by temperament but running on an empty pot, in a culture that has spent years draining it. Refilling it is
slow and cannot be mandated, which is why blameless process helps and blameless posters do not.

## The stances, learned as postures

The four survival stances are the book's most borrowed idea, and the [rest of the Satir work](satir-core.md) already
maps them onto auditors, incident reviews and policy. What the source adds is that Satir taught them as bodies, not
concepts. The placater half-kneeling and pleading, the blamer with a pointing finger, the computer gone cold and still,
the distractor in constant motion: she had people physically hold the postures until the feeling arrived, which is
[role-play](roleplay.md) in its oldest form. She also names the fifth, the one the other four exist in contrast to:
levelling, where the words, the face and the feeling agree. The physical framing is worth keeping, because a stance is
easier to catch in a stand-up or on a bridge call as a posture, arms folded, voice gone flat, than as an abstraction
lifted from a diagram.

## Seed against threat-and-reward

The New Peoplemaking sets two models of a group against each other. The threat-and-reward model runs on hierarchy,
compliance and the management of appearances: people do the right thing to avoid punishment or earn approval, and
information becomes currency to be hoarded or spent. The seed model treats people as growing things, oriented toward
learning, where difference is interesting rather than dangerous. Satir is describing families, but the security reader
will recognise both org charts on sight. A programme run on threat and reward produces exactly what it rewards: quiet
audits, agreeable stand-ups, and a workforce that has learned not to surface the thing that would look bad. The seed
model is not the softer option. It is the harder claim that an organisation learns only as fast as its people can afford
to be honest.

## What the New edition changed

The 1988 revision is more than a reprint. Satir widened the frame from the nuclear family of 1972 to the many shapes a
family actually takes, refreshed examples that had aged, and added material on the wider world the family sits inside.
The spine did not move: self-worth, communication, the stances, the two models. For a reader choosing between the
editions, the New one is the better bet, not for new theory but because its 1988 voice sheds a number of 1972 assumptions
that a modern reader would otherwise have to keep translating.

## Still a book about families

The examples are dinner tables and teenagers, not stand-ups and incidents. Satir writes with a warmth and a faith in
people that can read as naive to anyone whose working week is mostly adversarial, and the therapeutic register asks for
an earnestness the average security meeting will not supply. None of that sinks it. The book is making one claim, that a
group communicates the way its members were taught to survive, and once the claim is in the room it is hard to look at a
team's silences, its blame, the calm it reports, and unsee the stance underneath.
