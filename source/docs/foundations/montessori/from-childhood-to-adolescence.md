# Book review: From childhood to adolescence

![From childhood to adolescence](/_static/images/foundations-childhood-to-adolescence.png)

Montessori wrote comparatively little about adolescents, and "From Childhood to Adolescence" (1948) is most of what she
left. Its centre is the Erdkinder, the children of the land: a proposal for adolescents to live and work on a farm with a
shop and a guest-house, running something with real customers, real money, and real consequences when it goes wrong. The
educational content is not bolted onto the work; the work is the content. Of the books worth considering, this is the one
that ports to adults with the least forcing, because adolescence is the stage nearest to working life and because its
method is not simulation.

## Work that counts

The Erdkinder's wager is that a learner treats a task differently the moment its outcome is real. A ledger that has to
balance because suppliers expect paying, a crop that fails for actual reasons, teach in a register no exercise reaches.
This is the sharpest version of the argument under [hands-on work](hands-on.md): proximity to real conditions is not a
refinement, it is the mechanism. A security learner defusing a contrived scenario and one triaging a genuine, if bounded,
alert on a system someone cares about are not doing the same activity at two difficulties. They are doing different
activities.

## The stakes a game cannot fake

The book draws a line a good deal of security training walks up to and then declines to cross. A [CTF](ctf-value.md) is a
superb prepared environment, but its stakes are borrowed: the flag stands in for a consequence rather than being one. The
Erdkinder insists the consequence be present, which for adults points past the exercise toward apprenticeship on
production-adjacent work, supervised on-call, a [rotation](rotation.md) into a role whose mistakes are felt. None of that
is safe the way a sandbox is safe, and the book is honest that the discomfort is the point. Growth, in its account, needs
the possibility of a real result to push against.

## A sketch, not a system

The limitation is candid and structural: Montessori never built this. The Erdkinder is a proposal, a few dozen pages of
vision she did not live to test at the scale she tested the children's houses, and the schools that later attempted it
improvised most of the detail. As a source it inspires more than it instructs; a reader wanting a programme finds a
direction and very little apparatus. For security the caution doubles, because real stakes and inexperienced learner are
a pairing that can do real damage when the bounding is careless. The book supplies the nerve to stop pretending a game is
work, and a warning in the same breath: closing that gap safely is a design problem it names and does not solve.
